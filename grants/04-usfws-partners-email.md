I've been coordinating with Meridith Holm (USFWS Great Lakes Pollinator Coordinator) on native bird and pollinator habitat planning for these parcels, and she suggested I reach out to your office about formalizing a partnership through the PFW program.

**Project at a glance:**

- **Location:** 4475–4523 25th Street (odd side), Detroit, MI — nine contiguous lots totaling approximately 25,000 sq ft (0.57 acres)
- **Current condition:** Former residential lots, now vacant with a mix of mowed turf grass and early-succession volunteer species, including invasive Tree of Heaven (Ailanthus altissima), Common Buckthorn, and Spotted Knapweed
- **Goal:** Remove invasive species and establish native pollinator meadow, bird habitat, and riparian-edge plantings that support Great Lakes migratory bird corridors and declining pollinator species
- **Ownership:** Privately held by myself and seven co-owners; we are prepared to enter a 10+ year habitat conservation agreement
- **Cost share:** We can provide in-kind labor (invasive removal, site prep, planting, monitoring) and are budgeting matching funds from our LLC's operating account
- **Community monitoring:** We've established an iNaturalist biodiversity monitoring project (https://www.inaturalist.org/projects/dryad-25th-street-parcels-mapping) and are building toward a verifiable observation dataset for the site

**Why this project aligns with PFW priorities:**

1. **Federal trust species habitat** — The parcels sit within the Detroit River International Wildlife Refuge flyway corridor. Native plantings would directly support migratory bird species and declining pollinators including native bees and monarch butterflies.
2. **Threatened/declining species** — Invasive removal and native planting directly address habitat degradation for imperiled pollinator and songbird populations in the Great Lakes region.
3. **Complements the National Wildlife Refuge System** — Located less than 10 miles from the Detroit River International Wildlife Refuge, this project extends urban habitat connectivity.
4. **Habitat restoration, not establishment** — These lots historically supported native vegetation before residential development; we're restoring original ecological community function.
5. **Self-sustaining system** — Our planting plan emphasizes perennial native species adapted to local conditions that will be self-sustaining after establishment, with minimal ongoing maintenance.
6. **Reduces habitat fragmentation** — Nine contiguous lots create an unbroken half-acre corridor rather than isolated pocket plantings.
7. **Existing partnership** — Our coordination with Meridith Holm means technical guidance on species selection and planting design is already underway.

I'd welcome a site visit and the opportunity to discuss how the PFW program could support this work. I'm also in the process of applying for additional lots through the Detroit Land Bank Authority's Land-Based Projects program, which could expand the restoration footprint to 18 contiguous parcels (approximately 1.1 acres).
